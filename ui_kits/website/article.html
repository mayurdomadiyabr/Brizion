The case for retinal — and why we stopped using retinol.

A plain-language breakdown of why we picked the less-famous retinoid, how it converts in skin, and what 8 weeks of daily use actually did in our lab.

By Dr. Mira Chen Chief Formulator Share
Editorial cover · 16:9

Retinol gets the credit. It's the ingredient everyone names when they're talking about serious anti-aging. But when we sat down in early 2021 to build our first serum, we didn't pick retinol. We picked its less-famous cousin, retinal (pronounced "RET-in-AL"), also known as retinaldehyde.

Here's the short answer for why: retinal does the same job, it does it roughly ten times faster, and the irritation — the flaking, the redness, the two weeks of your face looking worse before it looks better — is dramatically lower. There's a reason it's the retinoid dermatologists reach for when a patient has sensitive skin.

Here's the long answer.

A retinoid is a retinoid is a retinoid (sort of).

Every retinoid in your bathroom is a form of vitamin A. To do the actual work — the collagen stimulation, the cell turnover, the fine-line smoothing — vitamin A has to convert in your skin into its active form, retinoic acid. That's the molecule your cells actually respond to.

The difference between retinol, retinal, and prescription tretinoin comes down to how many conversion steps they need to get there.

  • Tretinoin is retinoic acid itself. No conversion. It works the fastest — and it also irritates the most. Prescription only.
  • Retinal is one step away. Your skin converts it directly into retinoic acid.
  • Retinol is two steps away. It has to become retinal first, then retinoic acid.

Every conversion step loses some of the compound to metabolism. So if 100% of the retinal you apply could theoretically convert, only about 10% of retinol does. That's roughly where the "10× stronger" number comes from.

We formulate at concentrations that actually perform. If the data says 0.3% retinal gets you the same result as 2.0% retinol with half the irritation, the choice makes itself.
— Dr. Mira Chen, Founder & Chief Formulator

What the clinical looked like.

We ran an independent 8-week consumer study on the Firm & Lift Serum (our 0.3% retinal formula) with 84 participants aged 28–64. Applied nightly, with a moisturizer on top, no other actives in the routine. Dermatologist grading at weeks 0, 4, and 8. Self-assessment surveys in parallel.

96%said skin felt firmer
89%saw fewer fine lines
0%reported visible flaking

The last number is the one we care about most. Anyone who has tried a prescription retinoid knows the first two weeks — the tight, angry, peeling phase. That phase is the single biggest reason people abandon retinoids before they ever see the results. Zero reported flaking means this is a formula people will actually use long enough to work.

So why isn't every brand using retinal?

Two reasons, both practical.

First, retinal is harder to stabilize. It's sensitive to light, air, and heat, and left to its own devices it degrades into a less-useful form within weeks. Building a formula around it requires opaque packaging, careful pH, and antioxidant partners like vitamin E (which we use at 0.5% in every product partly for this reason).

Second, retinal costs more — roughly 4× the raw material cost of retinol. For a brand optimizing for unit margin, that's a hard sell. For a brand optimizing for whether the bottle actually works eight weeks later, it's the only real option.

We'd rather spend it there than on packaging.

End of essay
